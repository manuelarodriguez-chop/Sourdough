Room temperature guide
Below 18°C / 65°F — very slow, extend days by 1–2 20–24°C / 68–75°F — ideal range 25–28°C / 77–82°F — faster, watch closely Above 30°C / 86°F — feed more often, keep cool

A warm kitchen speeds fermentation; a cool one slows it. Never put your starter in the fridge during the first 7 days. The top of your fridge or near a warm (not hot) oven are ideal spots.

Wild Grape Starter
🍇
Wild Grape Starter (Levain Sauvage)
Italian & French tradition · takes 10–14 days

Unwashed grapes carry a natural coating of wild yeast on their skins called the "bloom" — the same yeast used for wine. By macerating grapes with flour and water, you capture this yeast and use it to build a starter. The result is a more complex, slightly fruity, wine-like flavour in your bread. It takes longer than a classic starter but many bakers consider it the most flavourful route.

Grapes to use
Unwashed red or white grapes — organic preferred
Why unwashed?
Washing removes the natural yeast bloom from the skins
Timeline
10–14 days total; grapes removed at day 3–4

0
Day 0 — The Beginning
Mix your first starter · takes 5 minutes

Every sourdough starter begins with just two ingredients: flour and water. You are not adding yeast. You are inviting the wild yeast and bacteria that already live on the flour and in the air to make themselves at home. This is day zero: the first mix. Nothing will happen today, and this is completely normal.

What to expect: A thick, paste-like mixture. No bubbles yet. Possibly a faint floury smell. This is correct — the microorganisms haven't woken up yet.

1
Day 1 — First Signs of Life
Observe · no feeding yet

You wake up and check the jar. It might look exactly the same as yesterday, or you might spot a few tiny bubbles forming near the edges or just below the surface. Either way: something is beginning. The wild yeast are stirring. The bacteria are beginning to produce lactic and acetic acid. The smell may shift from neutral to faintly sour or cheesy — this is progress.

What to expect: Possibly 1–5 small bubbles. A faintly sour or yeasty smell. No dramatic rise yet — that comes later. If nothing at all has happened, do not panic: cooler rooms take longer.
Observation log
No feeding today — resist the urge. Let the culture establish itself. Adding fresh flour now would dilute the microorganisms before they've had a chance to multiply.
2
Day 2 — Discard & First Feed
First discard · first real feeding

Today you meet the concept of "discard" — and it feels wasteful at first, but it's essential. You remove most of the old starter before feeding. Why? Because if you just kept adding flour and water without discarding, you'd have an enormous quantity of starter within days, and the ratio of fresh food to active culture would be off. The discard keeps the culture manageable and well-fed. (You can use discard in pancakes, crackers, and flatbreads — nothing goes to waste.)

What to expect: More bubbles than yesterday, possibly some rise and fall. A noticeably sour or tangy aroma. The texture may be looser. This is your culture becoming more active.

3
Day 3 — The Surge (or the Panic)
Discard & feed · watch for a false rise

Day 3 is infamous. Many starters go wild around now — doubling in size, full of bubbles, smelling strongly — and bakers get excited and think they're done. This is usually a "false rise" caused by leuconostoc bacteria, which die off quickly. Your starter isn't ready yet. Keep feeding. Conversely, some starters barely move on day 3, which is also fine — cooler kitchens are just slower.

What to expect: Possibly dramatic activity — or none. Both are normal at day 3. A strong, almost unpleasant sour smell is common and will mellow. Do not use it for baking yet.

Twice-daily feeding begins · building strength

Day 5 — Growing Confidence
Continue twice-daily · track the rise

By now your starter should be showing real, repeatable behaviour. Feed it, mark the jar with a rubber band, and watch. It should rise — doubling or more at its peak — then fall back down. Day 6 — The Float Test
Almost ready · do the float test

Today you can try the float test for the first time. Take a small spoonful of starter at its peak and drop it into a glass of water. Does it float? Day by day — grape starter method
0
Day 0 — Capture the Wild Yeast
Macerate grapes with flour & water

The grape starter begins differently. Instead of relying on yeast from the air and flour alone, you recruit it directly from the skins of unwashed grapes. The natural "bloom" — that dusty, slightly waxy coating you see on fresh grapes — is a colony of wild Saccharomyces yeast, the same species used in winemaking. You crush the grapes into a mixture of flour and water, and let the yeast migrate into the dough. It sounds ancient because it is.

What to expect: A thick, sticky, grape-tinged mixture. You'll see small grape fragments throughout. A faint sweet, fruity aroma. Nothing dramatic yet — the yeast are just beginning to colonise the flour.

How to crush: Squeeze the grapes directly into the bowl by hand — you want the juice, pulp, and skins all in contact with the flour. The skins carry the most yeast. Use a large glass jar or ceramic bowl. Cover with a cloth (not airtight). Leave at room temperature.

Grape choice: Red grapes tend to be more yeast-active than white. Organic or farm-bought grapes that haven't been heavily sprayed work best. Supermarket grapes work but organic is strongly preferred.
1–2
Days 1 & 2 — Fermentation Begins
Observe · stir daily · grape mixture still active

The grape mixture begins to ferment. You'll notice the liquid separating slightly from the flour, and a gentle fizzing or bubbling when you stir it. The smell transitions from sweet and fruity to gently alcoholic — like young wine just beginning to ferment. This is exactly right. Stir the mixture well once or twice a day to keep oxygen flowing and distribute the yeast.

What to expect: Visible bubbles in the liquid portions. A light, wine-like or yeasty aroma. Possible mild foaming at the surface. The grape skins will darken as they ferment.
Do not feed yet. The grape mixture needs 3–4 days to fully inoculate the flour. Stirring once or twice a day is enough. If you see white fuzzy mould (not just surface bubbles), discard and start again — this means the grapes were too heavily washed or treated.
3–4
Days 3 & 4 — Strain the Grapes
Remove grapes · first flour feed

By day 3 or 4, the grape mixture should be visibly active — bubbling noticeably when stirred, smelling strongly of young wine or fruit. Now you transition: you strain out the grape solids and keep only the liquid and flour mixture. This inoculated liquid is now your starter base. The grapes have done their job. From here you feed with flour and water just like a regular starter.

What to expect: The strained mixture will be loose and liquid-y with a strong fruity-sour smell. After adding flour it will thicken into a paste. It will look less active than before — this is normal, the culture is adjusting to a flour-only diet.

How to strain: Pour the grape mixture through a fine mesh sieve or cheesecloth into a clean jar. Press the solids to extract all liquid. Discard the grape skins and seeds.

Consistency: The final mixture should be like thick pancake batter. Add a splash more water if it's too stiff.
5–7
Days 5–7 — Daily Feeding
Discard & feed once daily · building strength

From here the grape starter is trained exactly like a classic starter. Discard all but a small amount each day and feed with fresh flour and water. The wild wine yeast gradually adapts to a flour-based diet while retaining some of the complex fruity character it brought from the grapes. Over these days you will notice the starter becoming more predictable — rising and falling on a cycle.

What to expect: Gradual strengthening over 3 days. The aroma softens from strongly fruity to a more balanced tangy-yeasty character. By day 7 you should see consistent doubling.

Days 10–14 — Ready to Bake
Float test · first loaf

Grape starters take longer to stabilise than classic starters — the wine yeast are more temperamental than the flour-adapted strains, and they need more cycles to become reliable. But by day 10 to 14, most grape starters are fully active and ready to bake. The flavour they produce is notably different: slightly more complex, with a faint fruity or winey depth in the background of your bread. Worth every extra day.

Ready when: Doubles within 6–10 hours of feeding (slightly slower than classic is normal). Passes the float test. Smells tangy, fruity, and pleasant — not sharp, alcoholic, or cheesy. Has visible bubbles throughout, not just surface foam.
Final readiness check
Grape starter vs classic — what changes in the bread?
Flavour: Slightly more complex, with fruity or winey background notes — especially noticeable in the crust.
Rise time: Often 10–20% slower than a classic starter — factor this into your bulk timing.
Crumb: Tends to be slightly more open due to the different yeast strain.
Maintenance: Same as a classic starter once established — fridge storage, weekly feeding.
Essential Sourdough Tips
Six things every baker should know
The essentials
🌡

Temperature matters

Fermentation speed doubles with every 5°C rise. At 21°C bulk takes 8h; at 27°C just 4–5h. Use a thermometer, not a clock.

⚗

Test your starter

The float test: drop a spoonful of starter in water. If it floats, it is active and ready. Feed 4–12 hours before baking.

🔥

Score deeply

Score at a 30–45° angle, not 90°. A sharp lame is essential. A bold score allows controlled oven spring rather than a blowout.

🍞

Steam is key

The Dutch oven traps steam from the dough itself, keeping the crust extensible during oven spring. Do not skip this step.

⏳

Wait before slicing

Let the baked loaf cool for at least 2 hours. The crumb is still baking from residual heat. Cutting early gives a gummy crumb.

💧

Hydration and flour type

Whole wheat and rye flours absorb more water. If using 20% whole wheat, increase water by 5% to maintain the same dough consistency.
